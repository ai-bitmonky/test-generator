D. $f = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{pE}{4I}}$

Solution:

Solution:
Strategy: Find the torque on the dipole as a function of displacement angle, use small angle approximation to linearize the equation of motion, identify it as SHM, and extract the angular frequency.
Expert Insight: A top student immediately recognizes this as a classic SHM derivation problem. They write $\tau = I\alpha$, use $\tau = -p E \sin\theta \approx -pE\theta$ for small angles, identify the form $\alpha = -\omega^2\theta$, and extract $\omega = \sqrt{pE/I}$. From there, $f = \omega/2\pi$ is immediate.
Key Facts Used: 1. Torque on dipole: $\tau = p \times E = pE\sin\theta$2. Rotational dynamics: $\tau = I\alpha = I\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}$3. Small angle approximation: $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ for $\theta \ll 1$4. SHM condition: $\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\omega^2\theta$5. Frequency: $f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi}$
  1. Step 1: Write torque on the dipole

    When a dipole makes angle $\theta$ with the electric field, the torque is:

    $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$

    $\tau = pE\sin\theta$

    The torque acts to restore the dipole toward alignment with the field (equilibrium position where $\theta = 0$). Therefore, the restoring torque is:

    $\tau = -pE\sin\theta$

    (Negative sign indicates restoring nature)

  2. Step 2: Apply small angle approximation

    For small oscillations, $\theta \ll 1$ rad, so:

    $\sin\theta \approx \theta$

    $\tau \approx -pE\theta$
  3. Step 3: Write equation of rotational motion

    Using Newton's second law for rotation:

    $\tau = I\alpha = I\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}$

    $-pE\theta = I\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}$

    $\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\frac{pE}{I}\theta$
  4. Step 4: Identify as SHM

    The equation has the form of simple harmonic motion:

    $\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = -\omega^2\theta$

    Comparing with our equation:

    $\omega^2 = \frac{pE}{I}$

    $\omega = \sqrt{\frac{pE}{I}}$
  5. Step 5: Find the frequency

    The angular frequency $\omega$ is related to frequency $f$ by:

    $\omega = 2\pi f$

    $f = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{pE}{I}}$

    This matches option B. ‚úì

  6. Step 6: Physical interpretation

    The frequency increases when:

    • Dipole moment $p$ is larger (stronger restoring torque)
    • Electric field $E$ is stronger (stronger restoring torque)
    • Rotational inertia $I$ is smaller (easier to accelerate)

    This is analogous to a torsional pendulum where $pE$ plays the role of the torsional spring constant.
