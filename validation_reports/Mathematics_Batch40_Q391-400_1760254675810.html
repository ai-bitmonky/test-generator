Question:

If $S_n = \frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2} + \frac{2}{n^2}e^{2/n} + \frac{3}{n^2}e^{3/n} + ... + \frac{1}{n}e$ then $\lim_{n\to\infty} S_n$ is

Options:

A. 1 ‚úì (Correct)
B. 0
C. $e$
D. none of these

Solution:

üìñ Complete Solution:
Step 1: Rewrite $S_n$ in summation form.
$$S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{n^2} e^{k/n} = \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{k=1}^n k \cdot e^{k/n}$$
$$= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{n} \cdot e^{k/n}$$

Step 2: Recognize this as a Riemann sum.
Let $x_k = k/n$ and $\Delta x = 1/n$. As $n \to \infty$, this becomes:
$$\lim_{n\to\infty} S_n = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{k}{n} e^{k/n} \cdot \frac{1}{n} = \int_0^1 x e^x dx$$

Step 3: Evaluate the integral using integration by parts.
Let $u = x$, $dv = e^x dx$
Then $du = dx$, $v = e^x$
$$\int_0^1 x e^x dx = [x e^x]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x dx$$
$$= e - [e^x]_0^1 = e - (e - 1) = 1$$

Answer: $\lim_{n\to\infty} S_n = 1$
